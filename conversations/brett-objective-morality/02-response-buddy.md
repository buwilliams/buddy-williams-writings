# Response to Part 1

Brett,

Thanks for sharing this, it's a rich piece and I've been chewing on it.

I find myself largely sympathetic to the framework, but I think there's a tension worth exploring. The argument draws a sharp distinction between subjective morality (grounded in feelings and psychological states) and objective morality (grounded in external facts about whether problems are being solved or exacerbated). That distinction does a lot of work in the piece.

But the author also invokes Popper's claim that we are all "equal in our infinite ignorance", and I think that principle, applied consistently, undermines the epistemological confidence the piece requires.

If our ignorance is genuinely infinite, then we cannot know whether any given solution to a problem is ultimately good or bad. It may appear to solve a problem locally and within our observable time horizon while generating worse consequences beyond it. What the piece calls "objective" is therefore more precisely described as local, provisional, and finite-horizon assessment. That may well be the best available tool, I think it is, but it's categorically different from "objective" in any strong sense.

To be clear: I'm not disputing that objective moral truth might exist ontologically. I'm disputing that finite beings can access it epistemologically. The ontology may be real; the epistemology is bounded.

I also accept that conjecture and criticism is the best error-correction process I'm aware of. But that process is itself a conjecture, and therefore equally subject to infinite ignorance. It's our best tool without being a guaranteed path to final objective moral knowledge.

The tension I keep returning to: the piece cannot simultaneously claim infinite ignorance and confident objective moral designation. One quietly constrains the other.
