# Objective Morality

## Overview

A dialogue between [Buddy](https://x.com/BuddyIterate) and [Brett Hall](https://x.com/ToKTeacher) exploring whether morality can be objective in the same sense as science or mathematics. The exchange centres on David Deutsch's principle of optimism — that all evils are caused by insufficient knowledge — and whether the framework of conjecture and criticism, drawn from Karl Popper's epistemology, can ground moral claims without falling into foundationalism. Key tensions include the rhetorical weight of the word "objective," the relationship between fallibilism and moral confidence, and John Searle's distinction between ontological and epistemological senses of objectivity.

- [01-transcript-brett.md](01-transcript-brett.md) ([video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oqtfB91i7Uw)) — Brett introduces the principle of optimism and argues that evil is a product of ignorance, not intent.

- [03-transcript-brett.md](03-response-brett.md) ([source](https://x.com/ToKTeacher/status/2032451878546722976?s=20)) — Brett rejects foundationalism, distinguishes knowledge from truth, and argues confidence has no place in epistemology.
- [04-response-buddy.md](04-response-buddy.md) ([source](https://x.com/BuddyIterate/status/2032595359256441279)) — Buddy accepts the Searle distinction but pushes back on the word "objective," proposing "intersubjective" as more honest.
- [05-response-brett.md](05-response-brett.md) ([source](https://x.com/ToKTeacher/status/2032703986432880653?s=20)) — Brett clarifies that "objective" simply means moral explanations are independent of how we feel about them.
- [06-response-buddy.md](06-response-buddy.md) ([source](https://x.com/BuddyIterate/status/2032825609840152648?s=20)) — Buddy asks whether "there is something to be wrong about" functions as a protected conjecture, and explores holding the status of moral knowledge itself open to revision. ([speaker notes](06-response-buddy-speaker-notes.md))
- [07-response-brett.md](07-response-brett.md) ([source](https://x.com/ToKTeacher/status/2036707562243149959?s=20)) — Brett takes Buddy's admission of being new to fallibilism as the central thread and lays out the trilemma: fallibilism, dogmatism, and relativism are the only three options, and relativism strictly contradicts fallibilism — you cannot blend them. Grounds moral objectivity in the multiverse: all physically possible choices exist, some are objectively better, and better moral explanations preserve the means of error correction.
- [08-response-buddy.md](08-response-buddy.md) ([source](https://x.com/BuddyIterate/status/2036785459628487117?s=20)) — Buddy accepts fallibilism and realism but challenges physicalism as a separate, unsettled metaphysical claim. Argues that Brett is epistemically open but ontologically closed, and that thoroughgoing fallibilism requires holding physicalism as a conjecture rather than settled ground. Uses biological sex and AGI personhood to show that objective facts about the current substrate carry less moral weight than Brett implies.
