# Response to Brett's Reply

Brett, I genuinely appreciate your response. I don't take that for granted.

Your reference to John Searle's distinction between ontological and epistemological senses of objective and subjective was particularly useful. It gave me a framework I didn't have before. I spent some time working through examples of epistemologically objective claims about ontologically subjective topics, and I think it's a genuinely powerful tool. For instance, "suppressing sexual orientation increases suicide rates" is an epistemologically objective claim about ontologically subjective experience. "Chronic pain reduces quality of life" is another. These are testable, meaningful claims about subjective states, and recognising that you can speak objectively about subjectivity without collapsing the distinction is clarifying.

That said, my main issue is with the word "objective" itself, specifically, that it hides the sense in which you mean it. You say confidence has no place in epistemology, and I agree. But "objective morality" functions rhetorically as a confidence claim, even if you insist it isn't one. When people hear "objective morality," they hear certainty, foundation, settled truth. The very things you're explicitly rejecting. The term works against the fallibilism it's supposed to sit alongside. And I'd apply this same concern to physics and mathematics. I wouldn't call them objective truths either. We can say epistemologically objective things about them, but not ontologically objective things. Repetition doesn't make truth, and our best models working spectacularly well is not the same as having accessed how things ultimately are.

I understand what you mean by it: that there's something out there to be wrong about, that moral claims aren't merely expressions of preference. But I think they are expressions of preference, and that's okay. Admitting that doesn't make them arbitrary or trivial. Some preferences are backed by our best explanations, survive criticism, and hold up across virtually every functioning moral framework. They're not "objective" in the sense of being verified against ultimate truth, but they're far more than personal taste. I'd call them intersubjective, for lack of a better word, and I'm not aware of whatever baggage that term might carry in philosophy. I just mean: the best we have, arrived at together, held provisionally. "Provisional" and "robust" also come to mind — they each capture a piece of it. And I think that's a defensible position. But I'd frame it differently than you do.

I imagine us as being plopped into an infinite expanse, no map, no instruction manual, and no compass but the one we build ourselves. That's us. That's the human condition as I understand it, and as I think you understand it too, given your appreciation of *The Beginning of Infinity*. We can't see the whole terrain. We can't know in any ultimate sense whether we're heading in the right direction. But we can look around, solve the problems in front of us, come up with our best explanations, and act on them. We can notice when something isn't working and correct course. We can be wrong, and we can do better.

That's enough. It's enough to oppose suffering, to oppose cruelty, to build something worthwhile, not because we've accessed some ultimate moral truth, but because we're doing the best we can with what we have, and we remain open to being wrong along the way.

Please let me know if I've misrepresented you or misunderstood. It's tricky to think well about the various distinctions and I look forward to getting a bit better at it. Important stuff!
