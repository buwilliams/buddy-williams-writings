Firstly, I reject the entire notion of anything being grounded in anything else. That's the way you're framing the way in which I was presenting morality. Morality is little more than "what should we do next?" That's the question. That's the fundamental sort of question, if you like. What should we do next? "What is happening now?" is the domain of science and whatever other domain of inquiry. "What should we do next?" is the domain that morality is interested in.

Now, why it's important to get that straight is because we need no grounding, no foundation. That's the error of foundationalism. It's like in physics — you don't actually need a foundation, and mathematics don't need a foundation. What you're doing is just solving problems, specific problems that come at you day after day, and perhaps the axioms over there in mathematics might help you, or perhaps these other axioms might help you. The foundations are not the thing that you have to rely upon as set in stone for all time as these eternal Platonic truths that allow you to solve your problem. That's true in mathematics, where it's very controversial to say such a thing, but it's certainly true also in science, and it's true in morality as well. You don't need these foundations, but that misconception leads to these problems.

Therefore, you have people like Sam Harris, and because he thinks things like science and mathematics are certainly grounded in an axiomatic foundation, that therefore we want to replicate that in morality. His idea of objective morality is to begin with a foundation, and that's when all the problems begin. I'm with him as far as there's an objective difference between right and wrong, good and evil, that kind of thing, but you just don't need to begin with a foundation. You rather talk about good explanations — good, fallible explanations. You can always be wrong about what you're fallible, the conjectural nature, the tentative explanations that you have.

For example, we can be objective, perfectly objective, about subjective states. In other words, this term, the objective/subjective distinction, has an ontological stance and has an epistemological stance. I can be perfectly objective when I say I like the taste of chocolate, or chocolate cake, or whatever. Now, the fact that I like the taste of chocolate — that is telling you about a subjective state of my mind. You might not like chocolate cake, so that's subjective, but it's a fact of the matter that I do like chocolate cake.
