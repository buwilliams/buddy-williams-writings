**Where we agree**

I think we're aligned on fallibilism, as long as we mean: knowledge and progress grow through conjectures and criticism, and we can be wrong about anything, including fallibilism itself. I share that completely. I also agree with Deutsch's point about preserving the means of error correction. If anything deserves to function as a near-universal moral principle, that does.

**The conflation I want to name**

Here's where I think the discussion has been running past itself. Fallibilism is an epistemological claim, one about how we acquire knowledge. Physicalism is an ontological claim, one about the ultimate nature of reality. You've been treating them as if they travel together, but they don't have to.

Your argument runs: fallibilism requires realism, because to be wrong about something, that something must exist. I agree. But then you make a second move, from realism to physicalism, as if the "something" that exists must ultimately be physical. That step doesn't follow from the first. Realism only requires that something exists independently of our knowledge. It says nothing about what the ultimate nature of that something is.

You can be a realist without being a physicalist. Many serious thinkers are. The question of what grounds reality, whether it's matter, information, mind, or something we can't yet conceive, is a separate question from whether there is a reality to be wrong about. I accept realism. What I'm questioning is whether physicalism is the right account of what that reality ultimately is.

**My position**

I'm happy to work within physicalism for the observable universe. It's our best current explanation of what we can observe, and I don't want to throw it out. But I don't think it represents the ultimate nature of reality, and that's not a scientific claim, it's a metaphysical one. The claim that physical stuff is ontologically fundamental, that physics goes all the way down, cannot be established by science. Science operates within the physical framework. It can't step outside to confirm that framework is all there is.

If I had to name my only metaphysical commitment, it would be this: reality is ultimately infinite. Explainable and subject to criticism at every level we can reach, but without a fixed ontological ceiling. I hold that as a conjecture, not a foundation. I notice that you hold physicalism differently, as settled ground. That asymmetry is exactly what I want to draw attention to.

For what it's worth, I think information is more fundamental than matter. Physical states are a subset of informational states. This lets me accept everything physics tells us about the observable universe while remaining open about what lies beyond it. Your multiverse contains all physically possible things. My conjecture is that physical possibility is itself a subset of something larger, all possible informational states. You've already stepped outside a single universe. I'm asking why you drew the boundary at physics.

So here's what I'd put to you directly: I'm not asking you to abandon physicalism. I'm asking you to hold it the way you hold everything else, as a conjecture, your best current explanation, open to revision. If you can do that, we may actually agree. If you can't, then you've found your foundation. And fallibilism, as you've described it, has a hidden floor.

**A concrete example**

You raise biological sex as an illustration of objective moral grounding, and I want to engage with it carefully because I think it actually demonstrates my point rather than yours.

I don't dispute that biological sex is an objective fact. XX and XY chromosomes exist, reproduction requires male and female, and no amount of cultural pressure changes that. Fine. Where I push back is on how much moral weight that fact is asked to carry. Objective facts describe what is, within the current substrate. They don't automatically tell us what could be or should be.

And the "what is" turns out to be more contingent than it first appears. The clownfish is a real biological example: when the dominant female of a group dies, the dominant male changes sex and takes her place. No engineering required, no edge case from outside biology. Sex, even within biology, is not a fixed structure of reality. It's a current solution to a current problem. And bioengineering extends this far further. The human genome has approximately 3 billion base pairs, each position one of 4 possible bases. The number of possible genetic arrangements is roughly 10^(1.8 billion). The number of atoms in the observable universe is around 10^80. The space of what biology could be is not just larger than what we currently observe, it makes the observable universe look like a rounding error. The male/female distinction describes a vanishingly small corner of biological possibility space, not its ceiling.

And if we step outside biology entirely, the point extends further. You also accept, as I understand it, that AGI can constitute genuine personhood. If that's right, then persons can exist without biological sex. Not metaphorically, not as an edge case, but as a straightforward consequence of your own position. Which means biological sex is a contingent feature of some persons, namely biological ones, not a universal feature of personhood itself.

This doesn't refute the objective fact. It limits its reach. The moral relevance of biological sex is substrate-dependent. It holds within the biological layer we currently inhabit. It doesn't hold across all possible persons, even within your own physically grounded framework.

That's the pattern I keep noticing in your argument. Objective facts about our current physical substrate are doing more moral work than they can support. They tell us how things are, here, now. They don't tell us how things must be structured for persons in general, or what moral frameworks should look like as the substrate changes. I'm not arguing against the facts. I'm arguing against treating them as a permanent ceiling on moral imagination.

**Where we actually disagree**

I want to name the disagreement as precisely as I can, because I don't think it's where you've been locating it.

I'm not a relativist. I accept that there is something to be wrong about. I accept that moral knowledge is real, that some choices are objectively better than others, and that conjecture and criticism is the only honest way to pursue that knowledge. We agree on all of that.

The disagreement is narrower: it's about physicalism as the ultimate nature of reality. You treat it as settled ground. I hold it as a conjecture, and I think thoroughgoing fallibilism requires that. Whether information is more fundamental than matter, whether physical possibility is a subset of something larger, whether the substrate we inhabit exhausts what reality can be. These are open questions. I don't have a worked out alternative ontology, and I'm not offering one. My position is simply that the question isn't closed, and that treating it as closed is the one place where I think your fallibilism has a hidden floor.

In practice, I'm not sure how much this disagreement would change anything we actually do. The questions I'm raising won't resolve themselves soon. We'd probably need to be deep into multiple layers of simulated reality before anyone makes serious progress on information ontology. And maybe information ontology is wrong too. I hold it as my best current conjecture, not a conclusion. What I'm confident in is that physicalism doesn't deserve the foundational status it's been given, and that keeping that question open is itself an act of fallibilism.
