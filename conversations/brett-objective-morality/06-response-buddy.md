# Response to Brett's Latest Replies

Brett, thank you again. Your willingness to engage like this is rare, and I'm learning from it.

I want to concede a point and then explore a question that's been nagging me.

**The concession:** You're right that the word "objective" shouldn't be a stumbling block between us. Your framing, that moral explanations should not boil down to how anyone feels, that they're tentative, working, subject to criticism, I agree with all of that. And I see the parallel you're drawing to science and mathematics. I share your process: conjecture, criticism, error-correction, no foundations.

**The question:** You say "there is something to be wrong about." I think that claim does essential structural work. It's what separates your position from relativism. You also say it's a conjecture, open to revision like everything else. But I wonder: could it actually be revised? If you genuinely entertained that there might not be something to be wrong about, the framework would lose its distinguishing feature. It seems to sit in a structurally privileged position. Formally a conjecture, but not really revisable without the whole edifice coming down. I'm not sure what to make of that. Is that a problem for fallibilism, or am I missing something about how conjectures can be load-bearing without being foundational?

**A model to make this concrete:** Imagine reality as layers of simulation. We live in one layer. Within it, there are facts we can be wrong about, problems we can solve, explanations that survive criticism. All of that is real to us. But from the perspective of any layer above ours, our "facts" are just the rules of our particular sandbox. Now imagine many such layers, each with its own inhabitants doing conjecture and criticism, each calling their local knowledge "tracking something real." From within any layer, it looks like there's something to be wrong about. But from outside, that something is just a feature of that layer's construction, not a truth that holds across all layers.

I'm not saying we're definitely in a simulation. The model just helps me see the structure of the problem. If "there is something to be wrong about" is always said from within a layer, and we can never step outside to verify it holds beyond our layer, then the claim is doing less work than it appears. It's a local fact dressed up as an unconditional one.

**Why this matters to me:** My concern isn't really about whether fallibilism is internally consistent. It's about creativity. Any moral claim grounded in our local layer of reality may prove too limiting. If we treat our best current moral explanations as tracking something unconditionally real, we risk constraining exploration. We might avoid entire paths, not because we have a better explanation, but because an existing moral consensus carries the weight of "objective truth" and discourages us from even looking.

Consider: there was a time when nearly every moral framework considered changing one's sex intolerable. Many still do. But as technology advances and our explanations improve, these boundaries shift. The same will likely be true of things we can't yet imagine. Forms of love, identity, and experience that current moral intuitions would reject. If those intuitions feel like they're tracking moral truth, they become stickier than they should be. They don't just say "this is our best explanation for now." They say "this is how things really are." And that closes doors.

To be clear, I'm not arguing for recklessness. I wouldn't violate a widely-held moral explanation just because I suspect it might be local. I'd want a better explanation first. But I want a framing that keeps the door open for that better explanation to arrive, one that treats even our most universal-seeming moral knowledge as provisional and layer-relative, so that when a better explanation does come along, we're ready to hear it rather than defending the old one as "objective."

And I'll be honest: calling it "objective" scares the hell out of me. Not because I think you mean certainty or foundation. I know you don't. And I think we actually agree that moral knowledge is objective within our local layer of reality. But the word doesn't stay in your careful hands. Someone hears "objective morality" and concludes: this is how things are, therefore it cannot be otherwise. That's the constraint on creativity that bothers me. Not that you hold the position, but that the language invites people to stop imagining alternatives. "Objective" sounds like a closed door. And in morality, closed doors are dangerous, because the things behind them might be exactly what we need to explore next.

So here's the commitment I'd make explicit: all moral knowledge, including its status as "tracking something real," is subject to revision. Not as a footnote or an implication of fallibilism, but as a top-level claim. If that revisability ever gets lost, buried under the weight of "there is something to be wrong about," then the framework has quietly become the foundationalism it set out to reject.

I suspect you'd push back in a couple of ways, and I want to think through them honestly:

You might say the same is true of physics, that "there is a physical world" is also a load-bearing conjecture, so am I a solipsist? I think I'd say: I hold that provisionally too. I think there is a physical world, and I act as though there is. But I try not to protect that conjecture from revision either. I'm not sure that costs me anything in practice.

You might also say that without something to be wrong about, I can't say cruelty is *wrong*. Only that I dislike it. But I think my framing does more work than that. I can argue against cruelty on grounds independent of anyone's feelings, grounded in explanations that survive criticism within our shared reality. That feels robust enough to do everything we need morality to do. It's just not sealed at the deepest level.

I don't think I've arrived anywhere final. I'm still working through this. But the conversation is helping me think more carefully, and I'm grateful for it.
