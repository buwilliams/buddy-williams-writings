**Brett Hall** (@ToKTeacher):

> We can use whatever words we like. If "objective" is a stumbling block then I'll just say: my point is that morality requires explanations that do not boil down to how you, me or anyone else feels in the main. Just as in science. Whether a moral explanation is good or not is *independent* of how we feel about it. No foundational claim being made, no "settled truth" and so on. I'm rejecting foundationalism in morality as I do elsewhere. Call it "objective" or call it "X morality" — it doesn't matter. It's just the denial of the claim it must be an axiomatic system. Rather than consider moral explanations "foundations" — consider them tentative, working, more or less useful, critiques of each other and of any given prescription about "what to do next".

**Brett Hall** (@ToKTeacher):

> Another analogous situation is epistemology. When I say it's "objective" I mean it's about things out there in the world: namely *objects* that contain knowledge or represent it and so on. Not subjective "beliefs" or some such.
>
> But "most people" think it is absolutely about beliefs and certainty and so on. Feelings and mental states. I consider that but a tiny sliver of what epistemology is truly about. That means I put even Rand's so-called "objectivism" in the subjectivist bucket too... precisely because it is subjective. It is about beliefs and confidence and so on. What "most think" has no bearing at all on the truth of the matter or which is best. After all, were it, we'd regard evolution by natural selection as false based on appeals to "most people".
