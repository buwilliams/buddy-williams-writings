# Stocks, Flows, and the Static Society

*On investing in skills, the cost of parity, and the thermodynamics of working at a firm that does not share your values*

**Buddy Williams** · May 13, 2026

---

## The Question

I started with what felt like a small question: what is the difference between a *milestone* and a *stock* in the way you invest?

A milestone is a discrete event. A point in time. You either hit it or you do not.

A stock — in the systems-thinking sense — is an accumulation. It has a level. It has a fill rate. And crucially, **it compounds.**

The reason this distinction matters is that companies, and people, often treat things that are stocks as if they were milestones. They assume they can decide to invest later, hit the checkpoint, and arrive where they need to be. But stocks do not work this way. Stocks accumulate slowly, and the rate at which they fill is bounded by something other than how much you suddenly care. By the time you decide to invest, your competitors have been compounding for years.

This was the question that surfaced everything else, because the firm I work at is making exactly this category error about AI.

## Stocks Are Not Flows

Skills are stocks. Brand recognition is a stock. Internal tooling, customer trust, talent networks, vendor relationships, organizational fluency, taste — all stocks.

The category error of a reactive corporate posture is to treat these as flows. **A flow is something you can turn on when you need it.** A stock is something that accumulates whether or not you are paying attention. You cannot fill a stock arbitrarily fast by deciding to care about it later.

When a company says "we will invest in AI when the time is right," what they are really saying is that they believe AI capability is a flow they can turn on. If they are wrong — if AI capability is in fact a stock that compounds with use, with experimentation, with embedded organizational habits — then by the time they decide to turn it on, the competitors who have been quietly accumulating will have built a moat that no amount of late investment can cross.

The compounding goes deeper than the first-order accumulation. Stocks generate their own flows. A firm that has accumulated AI capability has, by virtue of that accumulation, also built hiring pipelines, customer expectations, vendor relationships, and case studies. Those new flows further accelerate the accumulation. So the gap between leaders and laggards does not grow linearly. It compounds.

## The Fast-Follower Objection

A natural objection: **given enough time, all stocks eventually become flows.**

This is true. Literacy was once a rare, hard-to-acquire stock. Now it is near-universal flow infrastructure. The same is true of computer use, web development, basic data analysis. Technological progress converts stocks into flows by lowering the activation energy required to acquire them. If you take this seriously, the rational strategy is to wait. Let early adopters absorb the cost of experimentation. Adopt the commoditized version cheaply when it is ready. This is the fast-follower thesis.

The problem with the fast-follower thesis is that **the entire economic value of a stock is captured during the window before it becomes a flow.** Once a stock is commoditized, parity is freely available to everyone. So the fast-follower trades the possibility of surplus for the guarantee of parity. They never fall behind, but they also never get ahead.

This is a coherent strategy if the objective is to minimize downside, but it carries a hidden cost worth stating plainly: **you have chosen to never be behind at the cost of never being ahead.** No surplus capture. No competitive advantage. No participation in the upside. You exist in permanent equilibrium with the median competitor.

Three further problems weaken the fast-follower stance even on its own terms.

First, **not all stocks decay at the same rate.** Codifiable stocks — documented playbooks, factual knowledge, specific tools — commoditize quickly. Tacit stocks — taste, judgment, organizational reflexes, trust networks — commoditize much more slowly, because they are learned through embodied practice rather than transferred through artifacts. The fast-follower implicitly assumes the relevant stock is the codifiable kind. Often it is not. AI tooling will commoditize; *knowing when to trust an AI output, how to structure a team around it, what to ship and what to throw away* is tacit and slow.

Second, **commoditization moves the locus of advantage up the stack rather than eliminating it.** When literacy commoditized, the new stock became analytical writing. When web development commoditized, the new stock became product judgment. When AI coding commoditizes, the new stock will become architectural taste and problem selection. The companies that recognize *where* the new stock is forming are the companies that participated in the previous round. Spectators get the news late.

Third, **the recognition capability itself is a stock.** You can only see which stocks are about to commoditize, and which ones are forming at the next level up, by being a participant. Recognition is not free, and it is not bought; it is earned through prior involvement.

## Four Rationales for Parity — and a Fifth

If a company consciously chooses parity, the rationale matters. Different rationales have different stress points, and recognizing which is operating tells you what, if anything, can shift it.

The first three I could see clearly:

**Defensive logic.** The existing margin business is too valuable to disrupt. The board or owners have an explicit risk profile that prices parity as cheaper than leading. Internally rational even when externally suboptimal. This breaks when a competitor's lead starts visibly moving customers or talent.

**Hostage logic.** The decision-makers do not personally bear the downside of being late — tenure, compensation structure, retirement horizon — and do not share the upside of leading. Unfixable by argument. Breaks only on personnel change.

**Belief logic.** They actually think AI will commoditize fast enough, and the tacit-skill gap is small enough, that fast-follow works. Breaks when a peer company demonstrates the gap is wider than assumed.

For a long time I assumed my firm fell into one of these three. I kept making the strategic argument from an executive seat. I lost. Repeatedly.

The category I was missing — and that explains why my arguments never landed — is a fourth, and it is fundamentally different in kind:

**Identity logic.** The stance is not a bet at all. It is an *outcome* of optimizing a different objective function entirely. The firm is a **lifestyle firm**: it does good work, pays people well, provides a comfortable working environment, and is essentially indifferent to *what* it builds as long as it is software development. Its strategic identity is about the **rhythm of working**, not about the **work itself**. Investing in AI would change how the firm operates, and that change is precisely what is being preserved against. The strategy is not economic. It is existential in a small way: the firm is preserving its own DNA.

The first three rationales are all disagreements about means. They all want to win competitively; they merely differ on how. Identity logic does not want to win. It wants to keep being the kind of company it is. **You can't argue someone out of an end, only out of a means.** Investment analysis cannot move identity logic, because identity logic is not trying to maximize the variable that analysis measures.

This is why I had been losing the argument. I had been bringing strategy to a values conversation.

## The Lifestyle Firm as Static Society

In *The Beginning of Infinity*, David Deutsch draws a distinction that turns out to apply with surprising precision to firms.

A **dynamic society** is one that treats tradition as a hypothesis open to criticism, prizes variation, and accepts the disruption that comes with replacing worse explanations with better ones. A **static society** does the opposite. It suppresses variation. It protects tradition from criticism. It survives only as long as its environment does not change.

A lifestyle firm operates on static-society logic. Its feedback loops are tuned to "are we comfortable?" rather than "are we competitive?" Initiatives that would change how the firm operates are dampened — not because anyone explicitly opposes them, but because the immune system of a static institution is to absorb and neutralize anything that threatens the equilibrium.

There is a specific tell in my firm's stance worth naming. The leadership genuinely does not care what client requests come in, so long as it is software development. The firm has a thesis about the *rhythm of working* but not about the *work itself.* This is diagnostic. It means the firm has no organic defense against the work commoditizing — there is no "we are the firm that does X exceptionally" identity for clients to remain loyal to once X gets cheap.

The optimistic story lifestyle software firms tell themselves about surviving AI commoditization — "we will be the expert overseers of AI agents" — is thinner than it looks. Supervision capability scales with tooling faster than building did, because that is the *point* of better tools: the floor and the ceiling both drop. Clients hire firms for supervision only when the firm's supervision is *substantially* better than their own, and "substantially better" requires the tacit stocks the firm structurally does not invest in. So the firm's moat is being depleted *precisely because* the firm is not investing in deepening it. Even on the firm's own terms, the strategy is fragile. **The lifestyle is borrowed from a moat that is being drawn down.** The stability is real but finite, and the firm does not know how finite, because its feedback loops are not tuned to detect it.

## Lifestyle Versus Contribution to Progress

I have come to agree with Deutsch that progress through better explanations is life's central project. Knowledge grows through conjecture and criticism. The world becomes intelligible through the slow accretion of explanations with greater reach. This is not merely an intellectual hobby. It is, in Deutsch's frame, the thing that justifies the human enterprise.

I want my work — not just my life — to be meaningful in this sense. I want to be part of building something that grows what humans can do and understand. That is the objective function my values point toward.

A lifestyle firm's objective function points toward preserving the way of working. Both orientations are legitimate. They are simply not the same orientation, and the distance between them is not closed by argument.

This means my disagreement with the firm is **not a strategy disagreement. It is a values misalignment.** A strategy disagreement is about means. Two people who share the same ends can argue about which strategy best serves those ends, and the argument can be settled by analysis, evidence, and reasoning. A values misalignment is about ends. Two people whose objective functions point in different directions cannot settle the gap by analysis, no matter how good. Analysis is a tool for evaluating means against ends; it has nothing to say about which ends to prefer.

This reframing changed what I could even hope for. The firm cannot accommodate Deutschian values without ceasing to be the firm. That is not negotiation territory. That is definition territory.

## The Dynamic Pocket and the Extractive Firm

The reasonable-sounding move, given a values misalignment with the macro firm, is to carve out a **dynamic pocket inside the static firm** — a small zone, a team, a client engagement, an initiative that runs on dynamic-society rules even though the surrounding firm does not.

I have been running this strategy for a while. I do get to do edge research. I get to pitch new ideas. I am carrying one such initiative now.

But it is always uphill. Always unsupported. And after enough cycles I have started to recognize that the firm's posture is not merely *resistant* to the dynamic pocket; it is **extractive.** The firm takes the upside when initiatives land — revenue, reputation, the optionality of having someone who does this — without investing in the conditions that produce them.

This asymmetry has predictable failure modes.

The cognitive and political load compounds in *me*, not in the firm. The firm never develops the muscle. Every new initiative starts at zero institutional support. I cannot hand it off.

My initiatives stay in my zone, dependent on me specifically. They do not get institutionalized into how the firm operates. When I stop, or leave, the dynamic capability evaporates and the firm reverts to baseline.

Friction compounds into resentment that eventually bleeds into the work itself. Each cycle starts with less energy than the last.

The diagnostic question I now ask of any initiative I am carrying is this: **when (not if) it lands, what changes about the firm?** If the answer is "nothing structural — it stays in my zone," then the dynamic pocket is not actually working. The firm is using my energy as fuel while remaining structurally unchanged. That is not a dynamic pocket inside a static firm. That is the firm-as-funding-source dressed up as a dynamic pocket. The firm extracts my surplus and remains itself.

## Running on Uncoupled Energy

The deepest cost of working inside a values misalignment is not strategic. It is thermodynamic.

The energy required to invest inside a firm that shares your values is fundamentally different from the energy required inside one that does not.

In the aligned case, you are powered by the firm and yourself together. Your investment compounds with theirs. The work returns as much as it takes. The system regenerates.

In the misaligned case, you are powered by **yourself alone, against friction.** Every unit of effort you spend is partially consumed on overcoming the values gap *before* it can land on the actual work. That is an exponential drain, not a linear one. It is not a willpower problem. It is a thermodynamic problem. You are running on uncoupled energy, and uncoupled energy depletes faster than it can be renewed.

What depletes is specific and worth naming carefully. It is not motivation in general. It is the motivation to invest *in this firm* — the kind of motivation that requires shared values to renew itself. Other reserves — what I care about, what I would build given freedom, my appetite for hard and interesting work — are not running dry. The depletion is structural and specific, which means it tells me what is broken about the situation, not what is broken in me.

This matters because static institutions have a quiet way of making misalignment feel like personal failure. The signal that says *this is not my fit* is easily reinterpreted as *I am not trying hard enough.* Distinguishing the two is the difference between a useful diagnosis and a slow erosion.

## Where the Question Lives Now

A picture has assembled itself, and the picture has coherent implications:

1. The firm's strategy is values-driven, not strategic, which is why argument cannot move it.
2. The firm's stability is finite and depleting on its own terms, and the firm cannot see it depleting.
3. I have been the dynamic pocket inside the static firm, and the firm has been extractive rather than supportive.
4. My values point toward generativity and explanation-creation; the firm's point toward preservation of a way of working.
5. The energy I have been spending to keep this arrangement working is being spent against friction, and it does not renew.

I am not drawing a conclusion here. I am sitting with the implications.

But one thing has become clear that was not clear before. The energy the firm has been drawing down is *mine*, not the firm's. That I have been spending it here has been a choice, even when it has felt like an obligation. That fact does not tell me what to do next. It **relocates where the question lives.**

The question is no longer *how do I get this firm to invest.* The question is *what do I want my work to be, and where can it live such that the values and the work point the same direction.*

That is a question I can take seriously, because it is a question I am actually allowed to answer.
