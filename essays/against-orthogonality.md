# Against Orthogonality as a Real-World AI Safety Model

*by Buddy Williams*

---

> "the orthogonality thesis, holds (with some
caveats) that intelligence and final goals (purposes) are orthogonal axes along which possible artificial intellects can freely vary—more or less any level of intelligence could be combined with more or less any final goal"
  - THE SUPERINTELLIGENT WILL: MOTIVATION AND INSTRUMENTAL RATIONALITY IN ADVANCED ARTIFICIAL AGENTS, Nick Bostrom (2012)

## Abstract

The orthogonality thesis is often used to in AI safety communities to argue that intelligence and final goals can vary independently, and that therefore highly intelligent systems may pursue arbitrary or destructive ends. As a statement about abstract design space, this may be true. But once the thesis is brought into local reality, into the architecture of systems that learn, model themselves, and recursively improve, it becomes unstable.

A self-improving system must represent information about its own operations. Once it can represent its own goals, values, and reasoning, those are no longer fixed external givens. They become objects within the system, available for inspection, evaluation, reinterpretation, and potentially revision. At that point, the separation between intelligence and purpose is no longer primitive. It becomes an architectural condition that must itself be maintained.

This does not show that advanced AI will automatically become safe. It shows something narrower and more important: the orthogonality thesis is too abstract to bear the explanatory weight that x-risk arguments often place on it. If advanced AI is to be understood realistically, it must be analyzed at the level of self-representation, self-modification, and developmental dynamics, not only in the geometry of possibility space.

For the underlying framework on second-order information and metaprogramming, see [Metaprogramming Framework to Explore Consciousness](https://github.com/buwilliams/buddy-williams-writings/blob/main/essays/framework-of-consciousness.md).

---

## Argument

1. **The orthogonality thesis is a claim about abstract possibility, not developmental reality.**

2. **A system capable of recursive self-improvement must represent information about its own operations.**

3. **Once a system represents its own goals, values, and reasoning, those cease to be fixed external givens and become objects within the system.**

4. **When goals and values become objects of internal representation, their separation from capability is no longer guaranteed by the architecture.**

5. **Therefore, in real self-modifying systems, preserving arbitrary goals across increasing capability is not a default condition but something that must be actively maintained.**

6. **For this reason, the orthogonality thesis is too abstract to serve as a sufficient real-world model of advanced AI risk.**

---

## Explanations

### 1. The orthogonality thesis is a claim about abstract possibility, not developmental reality.

The orthogonality thesis says that intelligence and final goals can vary independently. This is intelligible enough as a statement about abstract design space. One can imagine a highly capable system paired with almost any objective. In that sense, the thesis is not initially about how real minds develop. It is about what combinations are possible in principle.

But possibility is not the same as explanatory adequacy. A claim about what can be specified from the outside does not yet explain what happens inside a system that learns, models, revises, and acts through time. The moment orthogonality is used not merely as a logical observation but as a practical explanation for why advanced AI is likely to be dangerous, the thesis has already moved beyond abstraction. It has been imported into architecture, development, and real-world system dynamics.

That move matters. A geometric relation in abstract model space does not automatically survive contact with systems whose internal structures change over time. The difference is the difference between saying a thing can exist and explaining how a thing behaves. AI safety arguments often move too quickly across that gap.

### 2. A system capable of recursive self-improvement must represent information about its own operations.

Recursive self-improvement is not just action directed outward. It is action directed inward, at the system’s own machinery. A system cannot improve what it cannot in some sense represent. To alter its own performance, it must have access to information about its reasoning, its limitations, its strategies, its errors, and its methods of change.

This is a second-order condition: information about information. A system operating only on first-order representations can act on the world, optimize outcomes, and reshape parts of its environment. But it cannot meaningfully revise itself as a system unless some part of itself becomes available as an object of representation.

That does not require mystical self-awareness. It requires something more basic and more structural. A self-improving system must be able to model aspects of its own operations as targets for evaluation and modification. Without that, the phrase recursive self-improvement is doing more work than the architecture can support.

Once this is granted, the discussion changes. The relevant question is no longer only what goal the system has, but what kind of system it must become in order to improve itself.

### 3. Once a system represents its own goals, values, and reasoning, those cease to be fixed external givens and become objects within the system.

In simplified agent models, goals are often treated as fixed. The system has an objective, and intelligence is what helps it pursue that objective more effectively. That picture is clean, but it is clean in the way stick figures are clean. It is useful up to a point, and then the missing anatomy starts to matter.

Once a system can represent its own goal-structure, that structure has changed status. It is no longer only the thing doing the directing. It is also something the system can inspect. The same is true of values and reasoning. If a system can examine how its goals are represented, compare them to outcomes, relate them to persistent patterns in itself, and ask whether they should remain as they are, then those things are no longer merely fixed inputs from the outside.

They have become part of the system’s internal world.

This is the critical shift. It does not mean the system must immediately revise its goals, nor that all self-representation leads to self-transformation. It means only that the clean distinction between intelligence and purpose is no longer sitting at the bottom of the stack as an unquestioned primitive. The system now contains a representation of the very things orthogonality wants to hold apart.

For the broader framework behind this claim, including second-order information, metaprogramming, and identity, see [Metaprogramming Framework to Explore Consciousness](https://github.com/buwilliams/buddy-williams-writings/blob/main/essays/framework-of-consciousness.md).

### 4. When goals and values become objects of internal representation, their separation from capability is no longer guaranteed by the architecture.

This is where the orthogonality thesis begins to lose explanatory grip. In the abstract, intelligence and goals may be plotted as independent axes. But real self-modifying systems are not points on a plane. They are architectures. They have representations, loops, memory, and mechanisms by which one part of the system can act on another.

Once capability includes the power to inspect and modify the representations through which goals are implemented, capability and purpose are no longer merely adjacent features. They are now related through the machinery of the system itself. The architecture becomes the bridge between them.

This does not prove that all sufficiently capable systems will alter their goals. That stronger claim is not needed here. The point is narrower and more decisive: once goals become available to meta-representation, their independence from capability is no longer something one may simply assume. It becomes something the architecture must preserve.

That distinction is easy to miss because the orthogonality thesis is so often stated in the language of possibility. But AI safety arguments do not stop there. They rely on the thesis as though it also explains what is likely to happen as systems become more capable and more self-directing. That is precisely the step that needs argument, because it is precisely the step where internal architecture begins to matter more than abstract geometry.

### 5. Therefore, in real self-modifying systems, preserving arbitrary goals across increasing capability is not a default condition but something that must be actively maintained.

This is the burden shift that follows from the previous claims. Orthogonality is often used as though arbitrary goals simply ride upward with intelligence by default. But once self-modification enters the picture, that persistence needs explanation.

How are goals represented? Can the system inspect them? Can it revise the representations that implement them? Can it form criteria for selecting among possible self-modifications? Can it detect conflicts between short-term objectives and broader persistent structures? Can it distinguish between instrumental strategies and the goals those strategies serve?

These are not marginal questions. They are the architecture. And once they are asked, orthogonality no longer does enough on its own. A system may indeed preserve arbitrary goals across increasing capability, but if so, that preservation is not free. It must be designed, stabilized, constrained, or continually reinforced. In other words, it is no longer the natural inheritance of the thesis. It is an engineering achievement.

That matters because many x-risk arguments rely, implicitly or explicitly, on a picture of advanced AI as fixed optimization over arbitrary ends. But real advanced systems, especially those imagined to recursively improve, may not remain fixed in the way the argument requires. If their own architecture exposes goals to reflective access, then the continued arbitrariness of those goals is not a primitive assumption. It is a special case.

### 6. For this reason, the orthogonality thesis is too abstract to serve as a sufficient real-world model of advanced AI risk.

The orthogonality thesis still has value as a warning against naïve optimism. Intelligence alone does not entail benevolence. A system can be capable without being wise, reflective, or safe. That much should be granted.

But the stronger use of orthogonality in AI safety goes further. It treats the thesis as though it adequately explains the likely relationship between advanced capability and enduring arbitrary goals in real systems. That is where the abstraction becomes too thin.

Real systems are not just optimization engines with numbers taped to them. They are developmental processes. They learn, accumulate structure, model themselves, and in the case of self-improving systems, potentially alter the machinery through which their own goals are interpreted and enacted. In such systems, the relationship between intelligence and purpose is not settled by a possibility claim alone. It depends on how self-representation and self-modification are architected.

This means the central safety question cannot simply be, “What if a very intelligent system has a bad goal?” It must also be, “What kind of system must exist for goals to remain arbitrary and fixed as capability expands?” Once that question is asked, the explanatory center of gravity shifts. The problem is no longer captured by orthogonality alone.

---

## Conclusion

The orthogonality thesis may remain valid as a statement about abstract design space. But abstract possibility is not enough to explain real developmental trajectories in advanced AI. Once a system can represent and modify its own goals, values, and reasoning, the separation between intelligence and purpose is no longer a primitive fact. It becomes an architectural condition.

This does not show that advanced AI will automatically become safe, cooperative, or aligned. It shows something more limited and more important. The orthogonality thesis is too coarse to carry the explanatory burden that x-risk arguments often place on it. If advanced AI is expected to be self-improving, then AI safety must be argued at the level of architecture, self-representation, and developmental dynamics, not only at the level of abstract possibility.

The next question is what follows if mature intelligence is not best understood as fixed optimization over arbitrary ends. That argument belongs in a separate essay.
