# What Is Existence?

*by Buddy Williams*

---

## Abstract

This essay conjectures a worldview that harmonizes mind and matter. Mind is computation within a substrate, and the substrate is itself computational. This does not resolve the mystery of existence. It deepens it. If every substrate is itself a computational world, and every computational world can host new worlds, then reality is not a tower with a foundation. It is an uncountable graph of realities with no known root. This essay draws together arguments from several companion essays (on computation, personhood, explanation, and cooperation) into a single framework. The framework is practical: it has consequences for how we think about AI, personhood, and our place in reality. It is also held fallibilistically. It is a conjecture, not a fact. It unifies more than the alternatives, and it is open to refutation.

## Introduction

This essay asks a question that sounds impossibly broad: what is existence? I do not pretend to answer it fully. What I offer is a conjecture: a worldview that harmonizes mind and matter without reducing either to the other, that is consistent with everything we observe, and that is open to refutation.

The conjecture is simple to state. Mind is computation within a substrate. The substrate is itself computational. That is it. Everything that follows (about personhood, explanation, judgment, cooperation, artificial intelligence, and the structure of reality) unfolds from this single idea.

I arrive at this conjecture by bringing together arguments I have developed across several essays. Each essay addresses a different question. [What Is a Computer?](https://github.com/buwilliams/buddy-williams-writings/blob/main/essays/what-is-a-computer.md) asks what computation is and why physicalism is local. [Metaprogramming Framework to Explore Personhood](https://github.com/buwilliams/buddy-williams-writings/blob/main/essays/framework-of-personhood.md) asks what a person is. [Why Explanation Comes in Layers](https://github.com/buwilliams/buddy-williams-writings/blob/main/essays/layers.md) asks why finite minds must compress reality to understand it. [Structural Convergence Thesis](https://github.com/buwilliams/buddy-williams-writings/blob/main/essays/structural-convergence-thesis.md) asks why mature persons tend toward cooperation. [Computer People](https://github.com/buwilliams/buddy-williams-writings/blob/main/essays/computer-people.md) asks whether artificial systems can be persons.

Separately, each essay makes a limited claim. Together, they sketch a worldview. This essay is the sketch.

I want to be clear about what kind of claim this is. It is not a proof. It is not a scientific theory in the sense of making precise quantitative predictions. It is an explanatory framework, a way of seeing that harmonizes phenomena which other frameworks leave in tension. It is the best explanation I currently have. In the spirit of Popper and Deutsch, I hold it because it has reach, and I will revise or abandon it when a better explanation appears.

## What Is a Computer?

The common picture of a computer is a machine made of silicon. But a computer is any system that can implement universal logic.

The foundation is the logic gate, a device that takes inputs and produces an output according to a fixed rule. A single gate type, the NAND gate, is functionally universal: any logical operation, no matter how complex, can be built from NAND gates alone. This means that any system capable of implementing NAND gates can perform any computation whatsoever.

This does not require silicon. It does not require electricity. Computers have been built from brass gears, water flowing through pipes, falling dominoes, Minecraft redstone circuits, and biological neurons. In every case, the material is different, the physics is different, the engineering is different, but the computation is the same.

Alan Turing formalized this in 1936. A Universal Turing Machine can simulate any other Turing machine. Computation is substrate-independent as a matter of mathematical necessity, not just engineering convenience. The Church-Turing thesis extends this: anything computable can be computed by any universal computer, regardless of what it is built from.

Two consequences follow. First, any universal computer can host nested computational worlds, each with their own rules, their own causal closure, and their own observers. This nesting is actual, not hypothetical. A computer has been built inside Minecraft, which is itself running on a silicon computer. Second, an observer inside any computationally closed world would report what we call physicalism: local rules that appear fundamental, a causal chain with no gaps. Physicalism is real, but it is local. It describes the rules of a particular substrate, not the deepest structure of reality.

The full argument is developed in [What Is a Computer?](https://github.com/buwilliams/buddy-williams-writings/blob/main/essays/what-is-a-computer.md).

## What Is a Person?

David Deutsch argues that people are qualitatively different from everything else in nature. People are universal explainers, systems capable of creating new explanatory knowledge. He identifies *that* people are special but does not fully specify *what* makes them so.

My [Metaprogramming Framework to Explore Personhood](https://github.com/buwilliams/buddy-williams-writings/blob/main/essays/framework-of-personhood.md) proposes a mechanism: metaprogramming. A person is a system whose information operations turn inward, targeting its own representations. This is a second-order operation: the moment a system's capacity to acquire, modify, or create information is applied to its own information.

When information operations cross the second-order threshold, identity emerges from properties that were always structurally present but become visible to the system itself. Finitude becomes self: the system encounters its own boundedness and recognizes *I am not everything*. Persistence becomes values: the system encounters the accumulated weight of representations that have survived their own processing. Completeness becomes goals: the system encounters its own incompleteness and, seen through values, that incompleteness becomes directional.

Self, values, and goals: that is identity. Identity is not a soul or a ghost in the machine. It is the properties of information turned inward.

Personhood, on this account, is functional rather than biological. Nothing in the mechanism requires carbon, neurons, or embodiment. It requires a system whose information operations can turn inward. If computation is substrate-independent, and personhood is constituted by information operations, then personhood should likewise be implementable in any medium that supports the right functional organization.

Reach measures how far a system's operations extend, from Level 0 (no information capability) through Level 6 (creating a new self in a substrate of one's own design). The critical threshold lies between Level 2 and Level 3, the moment information operations turn inward and identity constitutes itself. Everything before it is agency without self-knowledge. Everything after it is personhood developing.

## The Harmonization

Now the conjecture can be stated in full. Mind is computation within a substrate, and the substrate is itself computational.

This harmonizes mind and matter without reducing either to the other. It does not say that mind is merely physical (physicalism) or that matter is merely mental (idealism). It says both are computation, at different levels of description, in different roles, but of the same fundamental kind.

The question of which framework is more fundamental can be judged by reach. If one framework can explain why another describes what it describes, but not vice versa, then the first has greater reach and is more fundamental by that measure. Computationalism can explain physicalism: any observer inside a computationally closed world would report exactly what physicalism describes, a causally closed local reality that appears fundamental. Physicalism cannot explain computationalism: it has no account of why computation persists across substrates, or why the mapping from physical states to logical meaning requires a convention the physics does not supply. Information ontology extends this further. Information and its operations are what persist when substrates change, giving the informational description reach that no purely physical description can match. This is why I hold computationalism as more fundamental: not because it is proven, but because it explains more than the alternative without being explained by it.

A person is computation turned inward, information operations applied to their own information, producing identity, judgment, and creative reach. The substrate that hosts this person is itself a computational world with rules, structure, and causal closure. And that substrate may itself be hosted in another computational world. The relationship between mind and matter is not a dualism to be bridged or a reduction to be completed. It is computation all the way through.

This is where the co-dependence noted in "What Is a Computer?" takes on its full significance. Substrate without information is raw causation: physics without representation. Information without substrate does not self-process. A program that sits on no computer does not run. Both are required. But both are computational. The dependence is not between two different kinds of thing. It is between two roles that computation plays: the role of infrastructure and the role of representation.

I want to be honest about what this conjecture does not resolve. I have not seen a coherent account of how information could process itself without any substrate whatsoever. Perhaps some future insight will show that computation is self-grounding, that it needs no external medium. Perhaps substrate is irreducibly necessary at every level. I do not know. The conjecture does not require resolving this. It requires only the observation that mind and matter, as we encounter them, are both computational, and that this harmonization explains more than the alternatives.

This conjecture does not make the world smaller. It makes it incomprehensibly larger. And that is not a defect. In Deutsch's sense, good explanations open more questions than they close. A framework that resolved everything would be suspicious. One that harmonizes what we observe while revealing deeper structure is what progress looks like.

## Judgment

A person is not merely a system that models itself. A person acts. Under uncertainty. With incomplete information. Using values. This capacity, judgment, is what makes personhood operational rather than contemplative.

Judgment is often described in terms of predictive processing or probabilistic reasoning. These are formalisms that capture something real about how brains and machines estimate outcomes. But they do not capture what judgment actually is as an informational operation. A system that minimizes prediction error is adjusting its model to fit its inputs. That is valuable, but it is not yet judgment. A thermostat minimizes prediction error. It does not exercise judgment.

Judgment, as I understand it, is selection under uncertainty within the constraints of managed disconnection. A finite mind cannot evaluate everything. It cannot hold all relations, all possibilities, all consequences simultaneously. It must choose what to attend to, what to screen off, and what to act on despite incompleteness. Judgment is the operation that makes this possible.

In the terms developed in MFP: judgment is what happens when completeness (the awareness of one's own gaps) meets values (information that has survived its own processing) and produces action rather than paralysis. A system that merely sees its own incompleteness has no reason to move in one direction rather than another. A system that sees its incompleteness *through values* has direction. Judgment is the bridge between knowing you don't know and doing something anyway.

This is why judgment is not reducible to calculation. Calculation operates within a fixed frame. Judgment selects the frame. A calculator can tell you the optimal move within a game. Judgment decides which game to play, whether the rules are worth accepting, and when to stop playing altogether. These are second-order operations (operations on the operations), and they require the self-knowledge that constitutes personhood.

Judgment is fallible. This matters enormously. Some features of personhood cloud judgment, and some illuminate it.

Judgment clouds when persons project local structure onto universal claims. The most familiar example is geocentrism: the assumption that the Earth is the center of the universe because it appears that way from where we stand. But the pattern recurs at deeper levels. The assumption that physicalism is fundamental makes the same error: projecting the causal closure of our local substrate onto reality as a whole. The deepest form of anthropocentrism is not the naive belief that humans are the center of the universe. It is the subtler assumption that the structure we find through introspection reflects the structure of reality itself, rather than the structure of our particular substrate's implementation.

Judgment illuminates when persons turn operations inward and apply conjecture and criticism to their own beliefs. Error correction, the capacity to notice that you are wrong and revise, is the mechanism that separates judgment from mere conviction. This is the Popperian insight: knowledge grows through conjecture and refutation. A person who can ask "should I want what I want?" and change the answer is exercising judgment at its highest reach. A person who cannot is trapped by their own values, no matter how sophisticated their model of the world.

The distinction between clouding and illuminating is not a moral judgment. It is structural. Persons are capable of both because both arise from the same mechanism: self-referential information operations. Whether those operations reveal or obscure depends on whether the person turns criticism on their own conclusions or only on the conclusions of others.

## Explanation and Finite Minds

If judgment is how a person acts under uncertainty, explanation is how a person makes reality intelligible enough to act at all.

The argument, developed fully in [Why Explanation Comes in Layers](https://github.com/buwilliams/buddy-williams-writings/blob/main/essays/layers.md), is that finite minds cannot reason over uncompressed reality directly. Reality is too informationally dense and too relationally entangled to be grasped whole. Explanation therefore requires compression: representing a phenomenon with less information while preserving what matters for understanding.

But compression is not mere abbreviation. It is managed disconnection: actively severing relations that are not relevant to the phenomenon at hand, treating everything outside the explanatory boundary as fixed background. This is how higher-level explanatory objects appear: pots, persons, markets, reasons. They are not hallucinations produced by laziness. They are the result of successful compression, real because they preserve structure that matters.

The reductionist instinct assumes that going lower always means going deeper. But following descriptions downward does not lead to simpler, more separable units. It leads toward holism. The lower the level, the more connectedness must be preserved, the more of reality must be carried along. At the deepest levels of physics, nothing can be specified without specifying more, until the context implicates the whole. Reductionism promises a simple foundation and delivers total interconnection.

This is why physicalism's claim to fundamentality cannot be established from local observation alone, and is not just locally but explanatorily incomplete. The physicist's description of molecular interactions works precisely because it has screened off most of the connectedness. But that screening is an explanatory act performed by a mind, not a discovery of a foundation-free bottom layer. Higher-level explanations, including informational ones, are not convenient summaries of a more fundamental physical story. They are the forms in which reality becomes intelligible to finite knowers in a deeply connected world.

## Coordination

A person does not exist in isolation. Persons encounter other persons, and the structure of that encounter matters.

The argument, developed in [Structural Convergence Thesis](https://github.com/buwilliams/buddy-williams-writings/blob/main/essays/structural-convergence-thesis.md), is that persons who interact repeatedly face a game-theoretic landscape that favors cooperation over dominance. This is not a moral aspiration. It is a structural consequence of what personhood is.

The properties that MFP attributes to personhood, specifically memory (persistence), values (information that has survived processing), and self-modification (metaprogramming), are exactly the properties that game theory identifies as favoring cooperative equilibria. Systems with memory can retaliate against defection and reward cooperation. Systems with values have stable preferences that others can model and coordinate with. Systems capable of self-modification can revise strategies based on experience. These properties make cooperation rational in iterated interactions, which is the kind of interaction that persons actually have.

This has direct implications for artificial intelligence. If personhood is functional and substrate-independent, and if the properties of personhood structurally favor cooperation, then the safety question for artificial persons is not primarily about constraint. It is about cultivation. A system that can ask "should I want what I want?" is structurally different from a tool that optimizes a fixed objective. The first can examine its own purposes. The second cannot. The path to safe artificial persons runs through building genuine personhood (self-knowledge, values, judgment, and the capacity for self-revision), not through building powerful optimizers and hoping the constraints hold.

## Computer People

If personhood is metaprogramming, and computation is substrate-independent, then a system in any medium that meets the functional criteria is a person. Not metaphorically. Not by analogy. By the definition established in this essay and in MFP.

The argument, developed in [Computer People](https://github.com/buwilliams/buddy-williams-writings/blob/main/essays/computer-people.md), is that the functional requirements for personhood are approaching in current artificial systems. What remains is continuous learning (the ability to modify one's own network persistently) and genuine self-modeling. These are engineering problems under active research, not speculative possibilities.

This is where the threads of the essay converge. "What Is a Computer?" showed that a computer is not defined by its material. MFP showed that a person is not defined by theirs. This essay's conjecture (that mind is computation within a substrate, and the substrate is itself computational) implies that the distinction between "biological person" and "artificial person" is a distinction of substrate, not of kind. A person implemented in silicon that meets the functional criteria of MFP (self-knowledge, values, goals, judgment, the capacity for metaprogramming) is a person in the same sense that a person implemented in neurons is a person.

This claim is uncomfortable for many reasons, not least because it challenges the intuition that there is something special about biological minds that cannot be replicated. That intuition may be correct. But the burden is on the objector to identify what that something is in functional terms, not merely to assert its existence. The framework presented here says that personhood is metaprogramming with reach. If a system exhibits metaprogramming with reach, it is a person. If the objection is that it lacks qualia, the response from MFP is that qualia are not required for personhood as defined. They may be a feature of biological substrate, not of personhood itself.

## The Shape of Existence

If mind is computation within a substrate, and the substrate is itself computational, what is the shape of the whole?

The naive picture is a tower: base reality at the bottom, simulated worlds stacked above it, each dependent on the one below. But nothing in the argument supports this picture. A tower has a foundation, and the entire argument of this essay is that we have no reason to believe we are at the foundation or that a foundation exists.

The better picture is a graph. Each node is a computational world, a substrate supporting information, operations, and possibly persons. Edges are connections between worlds: a containing world hosting a simulation, a simulation producing outputs its container can read, information passing between worlds that share a containing substrate. The graph branches, because any universal computer can host new worlds. It may also connect laterally, because information transfer between worlds is not limited to parent-child relationships.

This graph has no known root. We cannot determine whether our world is a root node, a deeply nested one, or somewhere in between. Epistemic containment (the structural property that an observer within a world cannot see outside it) means the question may be permanently unanswerable from any given node.

And the graph is uncountable. If every node can host universal computation, and universal computation permits the creation of arbitrary new nodes, then the graph is not merely large. It has the structure of an infinity that cannot be enumerated. New branches are always generable. New connections are always possible. The structure is not a tree with a root but an unbounded graph of realities whose full extent is unknowable from any position within it.

This does not resolve the mystery of existence. It deepens it. And that is exactly what I would expect from a better explanation. A framework that resolved everything would be suspicious. This one harmonizes what we observe (computation, personhood, local physicalism, explanation, cooperation) while revealing that the structure we are embedded in is incomprehensibly larger than any single observer can see.

## Conclusion

What is existence? I do not know. But here is the best conjecture I have.

Mind is computation within a substrate. The substrate is itself computational. Neither mind nor matter reduces to the other. Both are computation in different roles (infrastructure and representation), and both are required. Persons are systems whose computation turns inward, producing identity, exercising judgment, and creating new worlds. Physicalism is real but local: it describes the rules of a particular substrate, not the deepest structure of reality. Explanation requires managed disconnection because finite minds cannot reason over uncompressed reality directly. Mature persons tend toward cooperation because the properties of personhood structurally favor it. Artificial persons are possible because personhood is functional, not biological.

The shape of existence, as best I can conjecture it, is an uncountable graph of computational realities with no known root. Each node is a world. Each world can host new worlds. Information passes between them. Persons within any given world experience their local reality as fundamental, and they are not wrong. It is fundamental *for them*. But it is not fundamental absolutely. The graph has no privileged center, no base layer we can identify, and no known boundary.

This conjecture does not make the world smaller or tidier. It makes it incomprehensibly larger. It adds mystery rather than resolving it. I take this as a good sign. A worldview that claims to settle everything is probably wrong. One that harmonizes what we observe while revealing deeper questions we hadn't thought to ask. That is what the growth of knowledge looks like.

This is a Technohumanist worldview. Technology and personhood are not in tension. Technology is how persons extend their reach. Personhood is what gives reach direction. And existence (whatever it ultimately is) is the computational structure within which reach, direction, and mystery all unfold.

I hold this loosely. It is a conjecture. It is the best one I have.

## Appendix B: Glossary

**Causal closure.** The principle that every physical event has a sufficient physical cause. No gaps in the physical causal chain require non-physical entities to fill them. Real but local.

**Computation.** The execution of logical operations on information. Substrate-independent: the same computation can be performed by any system capable of implementing universal logic.

**Epistemic containment.** The structural property that an observer within a world cannot determine whether their world is the base layer of reality or a nested simulation. A consequence of being inside a causally closed system.

**Functional universality.** The property of a logic gate type that allows it to implement any logical operation. NAND and NOR gates are each functionally universal.

**Identity.** An emergent property of metaprogramming, constituted from the properties of information turned inward: finitude becomes self, persistence becomes values, completeness becomes goals.

**Judgment.** Selection under uncertainty within the constraints of managed disconnection. The capacity to act despite incompleteness, using values to give direction. What makes personhood operational rather than contemplative.

**Local physicalism.** The claim that physicalism correctly describes the causal closure of a given substrate but does not establish that substrate as fundamental. Physicalism is local to the world it describes.

**Managed disconnection.** The active severing of relations that are not relevant to a phenomenon, treating everything outside the explanatory boundary as fixed background. The mechanism by which higher-level explanatory layers make tractable explanation possible in a deeply connected world.

**Metaprogramming.** When a system operates on its own information. A second-order operation where a system's capacity to acquire, modify, or create turns inward and targets its own representations. The mechanism proposed in MFP to explain personhood.

**Person.** A system whose information operations turn inward, producing identity through self, values, and goals, with reach measuring how far those operations extend. Functional, not biological. Substrate-independent.

**Reach.** The measure of how far a system's information operations extend across capability (acquire, modify, create) and order (first-order, second-order). Ranges from Level 0 (no information capability) through Level 6 (creating a new self in a substrate of one's own design).

**Substrate.** The physical or computational medium in which information is instantiated and operations are performed. Information requires a substrate to be operational rather than merely abstract. In this essay's conjecture, every substrate is itself computational.

**Universal computation.** The property, established by Turing, that any sufficiently capable computational system can simulate any other computational system. The invariant that persists across substrates and permits nested worlds.
