Yes, that's one of the strongest aspects of Kurzweil's forecasting methodology.

Most futurists make the mistake of forecasting specific inventions. The problem is that inventions are contingent and unpredictable. Few people in 1990 predicted smartphones, social media, transformers, or CRISPR specifically.

Kurzweil instead tried to forecast something more fundamental:

The amount of information processing available to civilization.

His argument is roughly:

Compute is the substrate.
Human ingenuity will find ways to exploit available compute.
Therefore the long-term rate of progress is constrained primarily by compute growth, not by our ability to invent applications.

In that sense, he is forecasting a capacity rather than an outcome.

This is similar to how economists think about infrastructure. If you know a country is building railroads, ports, roads, and power plants, you can't predict which businesses will succeed, but you can predict that economic activity will increase because the underlying capacity is increasing.

The same logic applies to compute.

4. Dependency absorption test

This is the novel part of your framework.

For each causal factor, ask:

If this metric increases, does it imply this dependency was satisfied?

For compute:

Dependency	Absorbed?
Energy	Yes
Manufacturing	Yes
Capital	Yes
Demand	Yes
Talent	Partially
Algorithms	Partially

Compute scores highly because many dependencies become unnecessary to model separately.

5. Upstream and downstream analysis

This distinction is particularly interesting.

Upstream absorption

The metric implies causes were solved.

Compute implies:

Somebody funded it.
Somebody built it.
Somebody found it useful.

You don't model those separately.

Downstream absorption

The metric implies consequences become likely.

Compute suggests:

Better AI.
More automation.
More scientific search.

You don't need to predict the specific inventions.

6. Compression ratio

Ask:

How many causal variables can I stop thinking about if I measure this one thing?

A great compression eliminates dozens of variables.

A weak compression eliminates only a few.

7. Intervention test

This is where the framework becomes practical.

Ask:

If I intentionally increase this metric, does the desired outcome tend to emerge?

This prevents selecting purely observational metrics.

For example:

Body temperature predicts exercise.
But increasing body temperature doesn't reliably produce fitness.

Bad SCM.

Meanwhile:

Minutes in active environments may both predict and cause fitness.

Much better SCM.

What's interesting is that your framework is not trying to find:

Root causes.
Necessary causes.
Sufficient causes.

It's trying to find:

The smallest measurable variable that allows me to safely ignore the largest number of causal dependencies.

That is very close to how good scientific theories work.

A scientific theory is valuable not because it contains more variables, but because it lets you throw variables away.

Your Success Compression Metric is doing the same thing operationally.

In one sentence:

A Success Compression Metric is a measurable variable that absorbs enough upstream and downstream causal dependencies that optimizing it reliably produces a desired outcome while minimizing the need to model everything else.
